    <p>Someone in your city searches "brake repair near me." They click on a result. They land on a page that says "We offer many auto repair services including brakes, oil changes, and more." They hit the back button.</p>
    <p>Then they find your competitor. Clean page. Clear headline. Explains exactly what's included in a brake job. Phone number at the top. They call.</p>
    <p>That's the difference between a service page that ranks and converts and one that does neither. Most auto shop websites have the second kind. Here's how to build the first.</p>

    <h2>Why Most Service Pages Fail</h2>
    <p>The average auto shop website has one Services page with a bulleted list. Oil changes. Brakes. Tires. Transmission. Maybe an image of a wrench.</p>
    <p>That page ranks for nothing. It converts nobody. Here's why.</p>
    <p>Google needs enough content to understand what you do and where you do it. One sentence per service isn't enough. And customers need enough information to trust you before they pick up the phone. A bullet point isn't enough for that either.</p>
    <p>The fix is simple: <strong>one dedicated page per service.</strong></p>

    <h2>The Right Structure for a Service Page</h2>
    <p>Every service page should follow the same basic structure. Not the same content, but the same bones.</p>

    <h3>1. A Headline That States the Service and Location</h3>
    <p>Not "Brake Services." Not "We Fix Brakes." Something like: <strong>"Brake Repair in [Your City] - Pad Replacement, Rotor Resurfacing & More."</strong></p>
    <p>That headline tells Google exactly what the page is about. It tells the visitor they're in the right place. It includes a keyword customers actually search. Do this right and you're already ahead of 80% of your competitors.</p>

    <h3>2. A Short Opening That Speaks to the Problem</h3>
    <p>Don't open with your shop's history. Open with the customer's situation. "Squealing brakes? Soft pedal? Your brakes are telling you something. Ignoring it turns a $200 repair into a $600 one."</p>
    <p>Two or three sentences. Hook them immediately. Show you understand why they're searching.</p>

    <h3>3. What's Included</h3>
    <p>Tell them exactly what happens when they bring their car to you for this service. Be specific.</p>
    <p>Not "we inspect your brakes." Instead: "We measure pad thickness, inspect rotors for wear and warping, check brake fluid condition, inspect calipers for leaks, and test brake line pressure."</p>
    <p>Specificity builds trust. Vague language makes customers wonder if you actually know what you're doing.</p>

    <h3>4. Signs They Need This Service</h3>
    <p>Add a short section on symptoms. "Your brakes may need attention if you notice squealing or grinding when stopping, a soft or spongy pedal, the car pulling to one side, or vibration when braking."</p>
    <p>This content is pure SEO gold. Customers search for symptoms, not service names. "Why does my car shake when I brake" is a search that lands on your brake repair page if you have that content.</p>

    <h3>5. Your Location</h3>
    <p>Mention your city naturally at least twice on the page. "We've been serving [City] drivers since..." or "If you're in [City] and hearing grinding when you stop..."</p>
    <p>Don't stuff it everywhere. Just weave it in naturally. Google needs to know where you are to show you to local searchers.</p>

    <h3>6. A Clear Call-to-Action</h3>
    <p>Every service page ends the same way: tell them what to do next. "Call us at [phone] or book online. We'll get you in today."</p>
    <p>Don't make them hunt for your number. Don't make them figure out the next step. Tell them directly. Visitors who have to think about what to do next usually just leave.</p>

    <h2>What to Do With Photos</h2>
    <p>Use real photos where you can. A photo of your team doing an actual brake job beats a stock photo of a random rotor every time. Real photos build trust. Stock photos make you look like every other shop.</p>
    <p>Name your image files before uploading them. "brake-repair-[yourcity].jpg" does more for your SEO than "IMG_4872.jpg." Add alt text that describes what's in the photo and includes your service keyword.</p>

    <h2>The One Thing Most Shops Skip</h2>
    <p>Internal links. When you write about brake repair, link to your oil change page. When you write about engine diagnostics, link to your transmission page.</p>
    <p>This does two things. It keeps visitors on your site longer, exploring your other services. And it tells Google how your pages relate to each other, which improves rankings across your whole site.</p>
    <p>A customer who came for brakes might notice you also do tires and call about both. That's a bigger ticket you would have missed with a single-page site.</p>

    <h2>How Many Pages Do You Actually Need</h2>
    <p>At minimum, one page for each major service category. For most shops that's six to eight pages: oil change, brake repair, tire services, transmission, AC and cooling, engine diagnostics, wheel alignment, and general maintenance.</p>
    <p>If you specialize in certain vehicles, like diesel trucks or European imports, those warrant their own pages too. "BMW Repair in [City]" is a search. If you have a page for it, you can rank for it. If you don't, you can't.</p>

    <h2>The Compound Effect</h2>
    <p>Here's what happens when you do this right. Each service page targets its own keywords. Each one is an entry point from Google. Instead of one Services page that ranks for nothing, you have eight pages each pulling in traffic for specific searches.</p>
    <p>The shop down the street with one Services page can only rank for "auto repair [city]." You can rank for "brake repair [city]," "oil change [city]," "transmission repair [city]," and five more. That's eight chances to show up instead of one.</p>
    <p>More pages also means more internal links, more content for Google to index, and more reasons for visitors to stay on your site. It all compounds over time.</p>

    <h2>The Bottom Line</h2>
    <p>A generic Services page is a dead end. It ranks for nothing because it's about everything. It converts nobody because it answers nothing.</p>
    <p>Dedicated service pages, built with the right structure, turn your website into an actual lead generator. Customers find the exact service they need. Google shows you to people searching for it. You get the call.</p>
    <p>Your competitors are relying on that one weak Services page. Take advantage of it.</p>
